Dining & Wine | Critic’s Notebook

Treasures on Every Block

By PETE WELLS MARCH 26, 2013

Continue reading the main story Share This Page
Continue reading the main story

RED HOOK IS A STRANGE PLACE to choose for a day trip. The nearest subway stop isn’t near at all, and it’s closed for repairs until next month at least. Driving to Van Brunt Street, the main drag, means a turbulent ride on 19th-century Belgian-block roads that are apt to end with no warning. None of the restaurants are on a fast track to the World’s 50 Best , and none of the food businesses, except perhaps Fairway Market, can be called a destination.

But for variety, charm, oddity and rugged urban beauty, there is nothing in the city like eating and drinking your way across this Brooklyn neighborhood. To understand why, imagine that what ran through Red Hook’s streets last Oct. 29 was lava, six feet deep in places. The neighborhood would have been a modern Pompeii of food and drink, a startlingly complete record of the things that obsessed hungry New Yorkers in 2012.

The buried machinery alone would have told a story. The vortex winnowers, conches and melting tanks would illustrate the making of chocolate bars from cacao pods. Copper-pot stills would document the rise of small distilleries. Steel tanks would record the fascination with orange wines and wild yeasts.

Coffee roasters would give evidence of New York’s pursuit of the perfect caffeine high. Sunk up to its engine in rock would be one of the city’s most avidly followed food trucks. Smaller objects like rakes and plastic vats would reflect other trends and newly revived traditions like urban farming, smoking and curing meats, pickling, and baking forgotten breads and desserts.

Of course, what really covered Red Hook was a flood tide swept in by Hurricane Sandy . Salt water destroyed much of that machinery, millions of dollars’ worth, not to mention all the food, alcohol and well-laid business plans. Five months later, after a herculean cleanup and with work permits still taped up in every third window, nearly every food and drink business in the area is open again. This is good news for them and great news for explorers with big appetites.

Instead of destination dining, Red Hook offers an impressive variety of tastes: dizzying lobster bisque, marshmallows you want to hide from your children, crunchy and spicy Korean pancakes, wines and liqueurs that are fascinatingly far from ordinary, and a potato-and-egg sandwich that should be on the curriculum of every Brooklyn public school.

Most impressively, Red Hook is a true community in a city where that notion is becoming more abstract all the time. Chefs buy from the local farm, and bartenders buy from the local distilleries; restaurants retweet other restaurants’ promotions; and shoppers talk about how happy they are to see the same woman restocking the bread at Fairway’s olive oil tasting station because the company kept employees on payroll while rebuilding the store.

“The sorts of people who fit in down here are the ones who understand that the local businesses aren’t in competition with one another,” said St. John Frizell, who owns a fine saloon called Fort Defiance. “What’s good for one of us is good for all.”

As New York’s dining scene has come to be dominated by empire builders, Red Hook has stitched together a bunch of little ideas that add up to something big, something that’s become hard to find in other places. It is the answer to those who see Brooklyn as a borough of overeducated pickle briners and amaro enthusiasts.

What follows are my picks for the best places to eat and drink in the neighborhood (open daily, unless otherwise noted). Buses run between Red Hook and Downtown Brooklyn, including the B61 along Van Brunt Street; New York Water Taxi operates a shuttle ($5 weekdays each way, free on weekends) between Pier 11 in Lower Manhattan and the Ikea store in Red Hook. My route has a winery at one end, an old Italian sandwich shop at the other, and in between, some more recent arrivals. Together, they make a destination.

Red Hook Food Vendors

True, it hasn’t had the same sprawling family-picnic feeling since the vendors were moved from the park to the sidewalk, and the lines can be purgatorial. But the food! When the vendors return in midspring, there will be warm and filling Salvadoran pupusas under a stack of shredded purple cabbage. From Mexico will come huaraches, chalupas, sopes and grilled corn on a stick. And from Guatemala, supersize tamales and chiles rellenos. There will be ripe mangoes and watermelon juice. Napkins will be soaked, and shirts will be stained.

Red Hook Recreational Area, 155 Bay Street (Clinton Street), redhookfoodvendors.com . Saturday and Sunday in season.

Fairway Market

The olive oils, cheeses and so on are approximately five times easier to browse thanks to a vastly improved layout (wider aisles, fewer bottlenecks). The outdoor terrace with its view of the bay looks better, too; the only thing that hasn’t been noticeably upgraded is the food in the cafe.

480-500 Van Brunt Street (Reed Street), (718) 254-0923, fairwaymarket.com .

Red Hook Winery

Drinking your way through six samples at Red Hook Winery’s tasting room offers liquid proof, in red and white, that the whole can be more than the sum of its parts. The winery is a two-pronged exploration of New York State terroir. One prong is named Robert Foley, and the other is Abe Schoener. The two divide each lot of Long Island and Finger Lakes grapes and go off in separate directions.

Mr. Foley ferments and ages wines with the precisely tuned techniques that have made cult items of his Napa reds. Mr. Schoener rolls the dice, inviting wild yeasts that ride in off the bay to do their thing. Sometimes he leaves the skins on as the grapes ferment to make so-called orange wines, which have some of the friction and flavor of reds.

Although I’ve had some Schoener wines that I would rather think about than drink, one that invites both is the 2010 Vipolze SK Reserve, a blend of chardonnay and sauvignon blanc with an amazingly rich body ($30 for 500 milliliters). With wines that range so broadly, tasting before buying is wise. And in fair weather, when the doors of the 19th-century pier are thrown open and you watch tugs and ferries crossing the harbor, it’s extremely pleasant.

Pier 41, 325A, 175-204 Van Dyke Street, (347) 689-2432, redhookwinery.com . Tastings, daily, start at $5.

Steve’s Authentic Key Lime Pies

Steve Tarpin is a pioneer’s pioneer. In 2001, when he moved his single-subject bakery to Pier 41 , Red Hook was on almost nobody’s map. His pies are still made with fresh-squeezed Key limes (there’s usually a bowl of them on the counter); they’re still a little sweet but not cloying, and they still jiggle. One change is on the way: Mr. Tarpin is moving to the pier next door. He hopes the bakery will be safer in the next flood; I hope he brings the appetite-stirring sign that reads, “PIES HERE.”

Cacao Prieto

This ambitious factory is attempting to treat cacao in a nose-to-tail fashion, turning pods from the owner’s family farm in the Dominican Republic into an array of products including preserves, soaps, a Nutella-esque spread, spirits and chocolate. Visitors can buy the products from a shop set up between a still and the chocolate machines. The rum distilled from cacao has an enduring coffee-like bitterness; easier to enjoy, on its own or in cocktails, is the Don Esteban cacao liqueur ($48), in which the chocolate flavor is balanced by a mild sweetness. The heart of the operation, of course, is chocolate. The 72 percent cacao dark chocolate ($10) is lush and extremely smooth without being sticky. No vanilla masks its emphatic, tightly integrated berry flavors. Both the pure bars and the ones with nuts and dried fruits offer a substantial hit of chocolate.

218 Conover Street (Coffey Street), (347) 225-0130, cacaoprieto.com .

The Good Fork

When New Yorkers pine for an intimate spot with neighborhood charm and a touch of romance, this is the kind of place they picture. Wherever you’ve just come from, the Good Fork offers a safe haven from it. There will be bottles from Red Hook Winery, cocktails made with local liquor, wedges of Steve’s Key lime pie and, as there have been since the opening night in 2006, terrific pork-and-chive dumplings in crimped, curved skins, like an open grin. They’re so well loved they may overshadow another appetizer, the crisp kimchi pancakes with a slow burn.

391 Van Brunt Street (Coffey Street), (718) 643-6636, goodfork.com . Tuesday to Sunday.

Dry Dock Wine and Spirits

Red Hook is home to one winery, a vermouth maker ( Uncouth Vermouth , just getting back on its feet) and three distilleries: Jack From Brooklyn , Cacao Prieto and Van Brunt Stillhouse . Only the winery and Cacao Prieto are open to the public; to sample the others’ handiwork, stop by this fine liquor shop.

Van Brunt Stillhouse makes a whiskey that needs a little more age, but its Due North rum ($39 for 750 milliliters) is ready now and has a honeyed sweetness reminiscent of Irish whiskey. Jack From Brooklyn produces my favorite local spirit, a liqueur called Sorel ($30 for 750 milliliters). A version of a home brew popular in the West Indies, it is an intense extraction of hibiscus flowers with identifiable notes of cinnamon, clove, ginger and other spices.

370 Van Brunt Street (Wolcott Street) until mid-April, while its permanent home is rebuilt: 424 Van Brunt Street (Van Dyke Street), (718) 852-3625, drydockny.com .

Fort Defiance

I doubt there is another restaurant in New York with a finer sense of what to drink when: a Ramos fizz with brunch, a Barbados buck with lunch, a Marconi Wireless (apple brandy, sweet vermouth, bitters) before dinner and an extremely good Irish coffee at any hour. The kitchen, under Steve Linares, makes pub food seasonal and contemporary without being obnoxious about it. On Monday nights, $12 buys a burger and a beer, like a Bengali Tiger I.P.A. from Sixpoint Brewery , three blocks away; the sticky toffee pudding is appropriate on any night.

365 Van Brunt Street (Dikeman Street), (347) 453-6672, fortdefiancebrooklyn.com . Daily except Tuesday night.

Baked

Behind the tangerine door with an antler for a handle, the mission is desserts from Main Street, U.S.A.: chocolate chip and oatmeal cookies; cakes and cupcakes; nut bars and lemon bars. The best of the bunch are the dense, dark brownies (sprinkled with salt, or not) that stick to your fingers and the almost incorporeal marshmallows.

359 Van Brunt Street (Dikeman Street), (718) 222-0345, bakednyc.com .

Home/Made

Inside their tiny kitchen, Monica Byrne and Leisah Swenson cook as if every meal might be your last; they don’t want you to go out without a smile. So the French toast is made with a buttery croissant, covered with a landslide of warm apple and pear compote, and ringed with caramel sauce. The room, done in flea-market chic, is small, but there’s free coffee while you wait for a table.

293 Van Brunt Street (Pioneer Street), (347) 223-4135, homemadebklyn.com . Brunch only, Saturday and Sunday, until later this year.

Red Hook Lobster Pound

If this place were on Cape Cod, I’d dream about it all winter long. You order by the lobster tanks, then carry your meal down the street to the dining room, outfitted with picnic tables and dangling lobster buoys. Traditionalists demand the cold, mayonnaise-enhanced Maine lobster roll, but my personal tradition runs to the warm Connecticut-style roll dressed with melted butter. Also excellent are the New England clam chowder, thickened only with cream, and the lobster bisque, which has so much sherry that I might have poured it myself. For dessert: a whoopie pie, filled with buttercream.

284 Van Brunt Street (Verona Street), (718) 858-7650, redhooklobster.com . Tuesday to Sunday.

Defonte’s Sandwich Shop

Nearly everybody who lives or works in Red Hook comes to Defonte’s, and every one of them will tell you that their favorite sandwich is the only one to get. Cold cuts are for first-timers. For regulars, there is the meatball hero; others choose eggplant Parmesan; my predecessor, Sam Sifton, advocates a variation: fried eggplant with mozzarella, hot peppers and mayonnaise. But I come here for the sandwich filled with a potato, egg and mozzarella casserole of surprising delicacy. It’s a direct line back to the Brooklyn that used to be.

379 Columbia Street (Commerce Street), (718) 625-8052, defontesinbrooklyn.com . Monday to Saturday.
